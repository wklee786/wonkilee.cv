Radio Relics and Cluster Mergers in Numerical Simulations

I am interested in exploring the formation history of radio relics and their connection to the merger history of the host clusters using numerical simulations. For this analysis, I have utilized the cosmological zoom-in simulation project, TNG-Cluster, to study the statistical properties of the merger-driven radio emissions (left Figure). Moreover, I also utilize idealized simulations, simulating cluster merger in a closed box, to understand the merger history and plasma acceleration mechanism that explains the observed system.

Self‑Interacting Nature of Dark Matter

I am now investigating the self-interacting nature of dark matter using merging clusters. As demonstrated in the Figure, merging galaxy clusters generate double radio relics propagating along the collision axis, while dark matter halos are dragged by the self-interaction and present different evolution compare to the cluster mergers with collisionless dark matter. I am leading the TNGC-SIDM project, where we simulate cosmological zoom-in simulations of cluster mergers with and without dark matter self-interaction with galaxy formation models and mass resolution identical to TNG-Cluster. The constraints on the self-interaction cross-section are estimated in parallel with multi-wavelength observations.

Cluster mass estimate

Cluster mass is an essential property for understanding the cluster features and the underlying cosmology. Although weak-gravitational lensing provides unbiased mass estimates, free from the assumption of hydrostatic equilibrium, complex mass distributions of the clusters can create systematics in mass estimates using WL analysis. I used numerical simulations and mock observations to analyze the impact of cluster evolutions in WL mass estiamte and so the underlying cosmology.

Relevant works:
WL mass bias — Lee et al. (2023)
